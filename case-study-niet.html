One Survey, Two Stakeholders: Designing Research That Serves Competing Needs

Overview

NIET partners with school districts across multiple states to support educator development through coaching, evaluation, and professional development programs. As a researcher, I design and manage large-scale evaluation research — including an annual partner survey distributed across states — that directly informs how the organization refines its services each year. This case study shows how I navigated a real constraint: two stakeholders needing data from the same population at the same time, with competing requirements for survey language.

The Work: Designing Research Instruments at Scale

Each year, NIET administers a partner survey to educators across its partner schools and districts to understand their experiences, measure satisfaction, and identify areas for improvement. The survey covers topics including professional development quality, coaching support, and evaluation system alignment. I own the annual revision process — updating content based on current literature, refining constructs to match evolving program goals, and ensuring question wording is clear and accessible for respondents across different state contexts.

This isn't a static instrument. Each revision cycle involves reviewing recent research on effective professional development, aligning survey constructs with what the literature identifies as meaningful predictors of impact, and pressure-testing question wording against how educators in different states will actually interpret it. The goal is an instrument that's both methodologically rigorous and practically useful — one that produces data stakeholders trust enough to act on.

The Challenge: A Competing Data Request

Midway through one survey cycle, a state education department partner approached our team with a separate request — they wanted to deploy their own survey to the same teacher population, at the same time our annual survey was going out. Two surveys hitting the same respondents simultaneously would hurt response rates on both and compromise data quality for everyone.

What I Did

1

Identified the Risk and Proposed Integration

Rather than simply accommodating the request or pushing back, I proposed integrating the state partner's questions directly into the survey I was already building. One combined instrument would protect response rates for both parties and produce richer data overall.

2

Built Internal Alignment

I brought the proposal to my supervisor first and made the case. With her support, I then presented it to the colleague who managed the state partner relationship directly. She had concerns about feasibility — I walked through the reasoning, addressed each concern, and secured her buy-in before anything went to the external partner.

3

Navigated External Pushback on Survey Language

The state partner wanted their questions worded exactly as they appeared in their official guidebook — their concern was that educators needed familiar, standardized language. I understood the reasoning, but the guidebook wording used context-specific jargon that would confuse respondents outside that state's system and compromise data quality for both parties.

4

Defended Construct Validity

I prepared revised question wording that preserved the same underlying construct — measuring the same thing — in language that worked across different educator audiences. I equipped my colleague with side-by-side comparisons of the original and revised wording, along with the reasoning for each change, so she could make the case in her conversations with the state partner. After careful review, they agreed.

The Outcome

For the State Partner

The state education department received actionable data on the specific topics they needed to evaluate — without deploying a separate survey that would have competed for the same respondents' attention.

For the Organization

The integrated findings fed directly into NIET's strategy planning for the following year, informing how the team refined services for partner states. One survey, two stakeholders with different goals — both needs met.

Methods & Rigor

Beyond the instrument design work, I conduct the statistical analysis that turns survey data into evidence stakeholders can act on. This includes the analytical work behind a peer-reviewed conference presentation at the Association for Education Finance and Policy (AEFP) 2026 annual meeting.

Key Learnings

Protecting Data Quality Sometimes Means Pushing Back

The state partner had legitimate reasons for wanting their exact language — but construct validity matters more than familiarity when you're surveying across different contexts.

Internal Alignment First, External Negotiation Second

I never would have succeeded with the state partner if I hadn't already built support from my supervisor and the relationship manager — each layer of buy-in made the next conversation possible.

Instrument Design Is Invisible UX Work

The same skill that makes a usability test protocol work — writing questions that capture what you actually need to measure, in language respondents actually understand — is exactly what survey design demands at scale.

Integrated two stakeholders' data needs into a single instrument across multiple states — protecting response rates, preserving construct validity, and delivering actionable findings to both partners.
